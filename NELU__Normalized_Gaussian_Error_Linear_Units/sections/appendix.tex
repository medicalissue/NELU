\section{Learning rate sensitivity}\label{app:lr}
%% ============================================================

We train ResNet-20 on CIFAR-100 with LR $\in \{0.01, 0.05, 0.1, 0.2, 0.5\}$, measuring final test accuracy.
If NELU is more robust to scale variation, it should be less sensitive to learning rate choice.

\section{Diagnostics}\label{app:diagnostics}
%% ============================================================

\paragraph{Gate entropy over training.}
We train ResNet-56 on CIFAR-100 with GELU and NELU, logging mean gate entropy per epoch:
\begin{equation}
    H = -\tfrac{1}{d}\textstyle\sum_i\bigl[g_i \log g_i + (1{-}g_i)\log(1{-}g_i)\bigr].
\end{equation}
By Proposition~\ref{prop:direction}(iii), gate entropy collapse in GELU corresponds to the output direction converging to ReLU's binary encoding.
We expect GELU's entropy to decline with training (as weight norms grow and the gate sharpens) and NELU's to remain stable (since the gate always sees unit-RMS input).

\paragraph{Weight norm vs.\ gate entropy.}
We plot $\|W\|_F$ vs.\ gate entropy per layer at the end of training.
A negative correlation in GELU confirms that scale drives gate collapse; the absence of correlation in NELU confirms decoupling.

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering

\paragraph{Stop-gradient (NELU $-$ sg).}
Removing the stop-gradient introduces the rank-one coupling of Eq.~\ref{eq:jac_nosg}, allowing each neuron's gradient to depend on all other neurons via the shared $\rho$.
If performance degrades to near-GELU, this confirms that the coupling is harmful and the stop-gradient is essential.

\paragraph{Normalization axis.}
For CNNs, we compare four choices: per-width ($\mathrm{dim}{=}{-}1$), per-position ($\mathrm{dim}{=}1$), per-channel ($\mathrm{dim}{=}(2{,}3)$), and global ($\mathrm{dim}{=}(1{,}2{,}3)$).
The global axis removes only the sample-level energy while preserving all internal structure (channel ratios, spatial patterns), and is used in all main experiments.
For Transformers, $\mathrm{dim}{=}{-}1$ (per-token) is the natural choice, analogous to RMSNorm.

\paragraph{Learnable temperature ($\tau$).}
A learnable scalar $\tau$ per layer replaces the data-dependent $\rho$: $z_i \cdot \Phi(z_i / \tau)$.
If the optimizer drives $\tau$ toward values that sharpen the gate (consistent with $\partial f/\partial\alpha \geq 0$ from Section~\ref{sec:consequences}), this confirms that data-dependent normalization is necessary.

\paragraph{Stronger weight decay (GELU + wd$\times$2).}
Doubling weight decay constrains $\|W\|$ globally, indirectly limiting the scale that enters the gate.
NELU removes scale from the gate \emph{only}, without penalizing useful weight growth---a more targeted intervention.

\section{LayerNorm does not prevent gate entanglement}\label{app:ln_ablation}
%% ============================================================

In a Transformer MLP, the pre-activation is $\mathbf{z} = W\,\mathrm{LN}(\mathbf{x}) + \mathbf{b}$.
LayerNorm controls $\|\mathbf{x}\|$, but the projection $W$ reintroduces arbitrary scale: $\mathrm{Var}(z_i) \propto \|\mathbf{w}_i\|^2$.
To confirm that LayerNorm alone does not resolve the scale entanglement described in Section~\ref{sec:direction}, we note that all gate entropy and corruption results in this paper were obtained \emph{with} LayerNorm (for ViTs) or BatchNorm (for CNNs) active.
The scale sensitivity arises \emph{inside} the activation, after the projection; inter-layer normalization cannot reach it.

\section{Computational overhead}\label{app:overhead}
%% ============================================================

NELU adds one reduction ($\mathrm{rms}$), one $\mathrm{sqrt}$, one $\mathrm{detach}$, and one division on top of the base $\Phi$ computation---all $O(d)$ and fusible into a single kernel.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{\textbf{Training time} (CIFAR-100, 200 epochs). N/G = NELU time / GELU time.}
\label{tab:overhead}
\vspace{0.5em}
\small
\begin{tabular}{lcccc}
\toprule
Architecture & ReLU & GELU & NELU & N/G \\
\midrule
ResNet-20    & 28.9m  & 30.1m  & 29.9m  & 0.99$\times$ \\
ResNet-56    & 47.0m  & 49.2m  & 42.7m  & 0.87$\times$ \\
DenseNet-100 & 109.9m & 135.4m & 171.8m & 1.27$\times$ \\
MobileNet-v2 & 34.0m  & 35.6m  & 47.4m  & 1.33$\times$ \\
ShuffleNet-v1 & 50.2m & 41.5m  & 42.7m  & 1.03$\times$ \\
ViT-Tiny     & 32.6m  & 32.6m  & 53.7m  & 1.65$\times$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Overhead varies by architecture: ResNets show negligible or even negative overhead (due to implementation variance), while DenseNet, MobileNet, and ViT-Tiny show 27--65\% overhead in our unoptimized PyTorch implementation.
The reduction ($\mathrm{rms}$) and pointwise operations ($\Phi$, division) are straightforward to fuse into a single CUDA kernel, analogous to fused RMSNorm implementations.
We expect a fused kernel to reduce the overhead to $<$5\%, as the dominant cost becomes a single memory round-trip (read $\mathbf{z}$, write output), identical to GELU.
We leave the implementation of a fused NELU kernel to future work.
